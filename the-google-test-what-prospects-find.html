The Google Test: What Prospects Find When They Search Your Name

By Anna Tran · March 18, 2026 · 5 min read

Here's something most business owners never think about: every serious prospect Googles you before they get on a call.

Not some of them. All of them.

Your Google results page is your real first impression. Not your website. Not your LinkedIn headline. The collection of results that show up when someone types your name into a search bar — that's what they judge you on.

And most business owners have no idea what shows up.

Run the Test Right Now

Open an incognito window. Type your full name plus your industry. Hit enter.

What do you see?

If you're like most service-based business owners, it's some version of this: your LinkedIn profile, maybe your company website, possibly a few directory listings, and then… nothing. Or worse — someone else with your name dominates the page.

Now imagine your prospect doing the same search. They're about to spend $10,000, $50,000, maybe $200,000 with you. They want reassurance. They want proof that you're real, that you're credible, that other people have validated your expertise.

And all they find is a LinkedIn profile you updated six months ago.

That gap between what they need to see and what they actually find? That's costing you deals right now.

What a Strong Google Presence Actually Looks Like

When a prospect searches someone with real authority positioning, the results tell a story:

  • Media features — Articles in recognized publications where they're quoted or profiled
  • Published content — Bylined articles that demonstrate deep expertise
  • Interviews and podcasts — Third-party platforms that chose to feature them
  • Consistent messaging — Every result reinforces the same positioning
  • Social proof at scale — Not just testimonials on their own site, but external validation

This doesn't happen by accident. It's engineered through strategic PR.

Why Your Website Alone Isn't Enough

A lot of business owners think a well-designed website solves the credibility problem. It doesn't.

Your website is owned media. You control every word on it. Prospects know that. They discount it accordingly — not consciously, but instinctively. "Of course their website says they're great. Everyone's website says that."

Media features are different. When Forbes, Business Insider, or even a respected industry publication features you, there's an implicit editorial endorsement. Someone with no financial incentive decided your expertise was worth sharing with their audience.

That carries weight your website never will.

The Compound Effect of PR on Search Results

One media feature is good. Five is transformative. Here's why.

Each placement creates a new indexed page that Google associates with your name. Over time, your search results shift from "person with a LinkedIn" to "recognized authority with media validation." And this compounds — each new feature reinforces the others.

Six months of consistent PR can completely reshape what prospects find when they search for you. And unlike paid ads, these results are permanent. They keep working years after the original placement.

The Sales Impact Is Measurable

Business owners who build strong Google presence through PR consistently report:

  • Prospects arriving to calls already convinced (not skeptical)
  • Pricing objections dropping by 40-60%
  • Referral partners citing media features as reason for introduction
  • Inbound inquiries from people who "found them in an article"
  • Competitors losing deals specifically because they lack the same credibility signals

This isn't abstract brand-building. It's measurable revenue impact.

What It Takes to Fix Your Google Results

The process isn't complicated, but it requires strategy:

  1. Audit your current results — Know exactly what prospects see today
  2. Define your authority angle — What specific expertise do you want to be known for?
  3. Target the right outlets — Publications your ideal clients actually read and respect
  4. Create a placement cadence — Consistent features over 3-6 months, not a single hit
  5. Integrate with your sales process — Link features in email signatures, proposals, and follow-ups

Your prospects are already Googling you. The only question is what they find.

You can leave it to chance and hope your LinkedIn profile is convincing enough. Or you can engineer your search results to tell the story of an established, media-validated authority.
